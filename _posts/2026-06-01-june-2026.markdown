# The Master's Post-Mortem

There are a couple of ways to evaluate whether getting a master's degree was a mistake or not.

The first way is to assess whether I accomplished the goals I set for myself *a priori* while in the master's program. I'd say the results on this one are mixed. My two motives for doing the MS in Data Science at UChicago were:

1. its research focus, especially because I didn't have any fulfilling research experience as an undergrad, and
2. landing an industry job as an applied scientist or ML researcher, which doing academic research in a graduate program would be in service of.

I was luckily able to land the applied scientist job I coveted, but I can't say my research experience at UChicago amounted to anything at all. In fact, I grew to actively dislike the research project I joined, mostly because of my own technical shortcomings, my poor project fit and lack of passion for that particular problem space, and some struggles in my personal life that impeded my ability to produce quality work. But on the whole, doing research was always slightly incongruent with my industry-focused goals anyway, and so the fact that I was able to accomplish (2) is far more important than my shortcomings in (1). In this narrow sense, it's hard to call the master's a mistake, because I got basically exactly what I wanted out of it.

I think this way of evaluating whether doing a master's was a mistake is wrong though.

Asking whether I accomplished my goals within the program begs the question on whether doing a master's was the right vehicle for those goals in the first place. The more important question is what the counterfactual to doing the UChicago MSDS was, and whether I would have been able to accomplish my goals in any of those alternate universes. Last year, as I was evaluating which master's program to choose, my focus was on questions like "is a master's in data science a recruitment liability compared to a master's in computer science," or "how is doing research aligned with my career goals." In hindsight, these questions only mattered on the margin, and I'd say I made the correct decision by picking UChicago solely based on the fact that I was able to get out of master's hell in one year as opposed to two.

The real counterfactual I should have been evaluating against is whether I should have done a master's degree at all.

I think reducing the merits of these ideas to a cost-benefit analysis of impact-on-career-earnings is a bit crude and myopic, but it's still a useful exercise in so far as it evaluates the decision quality against something measurable.
1. Without a Master's degree, my return offer from Workday would have put me at a total compensation of $XYZ. With a master's degree, they were offering a wage premium around 40% higher than the level 1 new grad position.
2. My base offer from Uber is around 30% higher than my $XYZ TC at Workday, but performance-based bonuses could put the wage premium as high as 50%.
3. Once I deduct the cost of my master's program from my Workday and Uber offers, I come out to anywhere from 10 to 20% higher TC.

So in a pecuniary sense, the math works out that the master's was a financial net positive.

The problem with this analysis though is that it assumes that I wouldn't have gotten my Uber offer – or hell, another comparable applied science offer – without a master's degree, and I'm not sure that's true.

Anecdotally, I did feel as though having the master's on my resume gave me a boost during this recruitment cycle. I got more interviews than ever before, more offers than ever before, and some of those offers were for master's-specific roles. So there is *some* tangible benefit to having the credential, provided the costs aren't too high.

But "do a master's" and "don't do a master's" aren't the only two options, and that's where I think I failed to be sufficiently imaginative. A paid pre-doc or research assistant position could plausibly have satisfied both of my original goals –– research experience and a path into applied science –– at zero financial cost, and without spending nine months away from my friends and family in a city I didn't particularly want to live in.

Part of why I didn't seriously consider this at the time was that pre-docs carry an implicit assumption that you're interested in a PhD, which I wasn't, and I still think that's generally the right heuristic. But I've since come around to the view that pre-docs are undersold as an option even for people who are on the fence. At Stanford, for example, pre-docs are paid positions that come with access to coursework, so you're getting research experience, a living wage, and academic exposure without committing to a PhD at the end. In hindsight, an econometrics-focused pre-doc in particular would have been at least as useful a signal for my Uber role as my master's was, probably more so given how central causal inference is to the work, and without the significant financial or personal cost.

In short, the scope of universes in which I could have accomplished my goals is much larger than what I had considered at the time.

As far as any advice I can offer to people thinking about a master's degree (mostly tailored for the tech space):

1. Clearly think about what your goals are (e.g., pivoting from one industry to another, going further into academia with a Ph.D., access to roles that largely seem to require a graduate degree).
2. Stress-test whether a master's degree is the only path to those same goals, or just the most obvious one; if your goal is to become an AI researcher and you did math or CS in your undergrad, for example, then an RA position and networking at conferences is probably a better use of your time than a master's if you're looking for more research experience (take an extra semester of undergrad if you really need to).
3. If you do conclude a master's makes sense, find the most frictionless version of it. I'm not saying "just do an online master's," since there's a real tradeoff between selectivity and signaling value, but programs like <a href="https://omscs.gatech.edu/">Georgia Tech's OMSCS</a> exist as low-cost, low-friction options and are worth taking seriously if the credential is what you're after.

For me, my master's degree was almost entirely signaling. Sure, I did learn new things through coursework, but the marginal value of that knowledge was modest at this point in my education and probably not much different from staying in undergrad an extra semester or two. I do not feel deeply enriched or significantly smarter after doing my master's, and in all honesty, the skills that got me my job at Uber are things I've known since my sophomore year of college. The degree opened doors, but it wasn't a free lunch, both in a financial sense and also in a personal sense. I really did not like being away from my entire social network in the Bay Area for nine months, to the point where I was so thrilled to leave Hyde Park that I didn't even bother staying for my graduation ceremony. I'm genuinely frustrated that industry still seems to cling to shallow credentialism and gatekeeps DS/ML roles behind what I think are unnecessary degrees, and I hope that the "just do things" mentality eventually becomes more valued than vapid signaling.

*Obligatory caveat: everything here is based on my own experiences and particular pathologies, and I'm probably not the most objective narrator. I know plenty of people who loved their master's programs and came out the other side with more to show for it than I did.*
